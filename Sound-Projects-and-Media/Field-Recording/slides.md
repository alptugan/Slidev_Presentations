# What is Field Recording?

**Field recording** is the practice of capturing audio outside of a controlled studio environment.

It involves:
- Recording natural environments (forests, oceans, cities)
- Capturing specific sound events and actions
- Documenting acoustic spaces and their unique characteristics
- Creating original sound libraries for media production

> *"The microphone is your ear in the field"*

# Categories of Field Recordings

In sound design, you categorize field recordings based on their function in your final mix:

::left::

## 1. Ambiences (Atmos)

Background sounds that define a location:
- Low hum of a city
- Rustle of leaves in a forest
- "Room tone" of an empty hallway

## 2. Discrete Sound Effects (SFX)

Specific, isolated sounds:
- Car door slamming
- Bird chirping
- Footsteps on gravel

::right::

## 3. Found Sounds

Using everyday objects in the field to create musical or abstract textures:

| Object | Sound Application |
|--------|-------------------|
| Metal fence | Cinematic "thud" |
| Glass bottles | Percussive tones |
| Rocks scraping | Tension textures |
| Water droplets | Rhythmic elements |

<!--
These categories help sound designers organize their recordings and understand how they'll be used in post-production.
-->

# Historical Overview: Early Beginnings

- **1890s** - Thomas Edison's phonograph enables the first sound recordings
- **1920s** - Optical film recording allows sound to accompany cinema
- **1940s** - Pierre Schaeffer pioneers **Musique Concrète** in Paris
  - First to use field recordings as compositional material
  - Created the "Studio d'Essai" at Radiodiffusion Française
- **1950s-60s** - Electroacoustic studios emerge globally
  - GRM (Groupe de Recherches Musicales) founded by Schaeffer
  - WDR Studio in Cologne (Stockhausen)

<!--
The history of field recording is deeply intertwined with the development of recording technology and experimental music. Pierre Schaeffer's work fundamentally changed how we think about recorded sound.
-->

# Case Study 1: Pierre Schaeffer
## The Father of Musique Concrète

- **1948** - Composed "Étude aux chemins de fer" (Study with Railroads), [listen](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=N9pOq8u6-bA)
  - First piece using train sounds as musical material
- Developed **sampling** and **tape manipulation** techniques
- Created the concept of **"sound object"** (objet sonore)
- Influenced entire generations of electronic musicians

**Key Works:**
- *Cinq études de bruits* (Five Studies of Noises) - 1948
- *Symphonie pour un homme seul* - 1950
- *Étude aux objets* - 1959

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-900 rounded text-xs">
<strong>Listen:</strong> "Étude aux chemins de fer" on YouTube - composed entirely from train station recordings
</div>

# Microphone Patterns

Microphone polar patterns determine what sounds are captured and from which direction:

::left::

## Omnidirectional
- Captures sound from **all directions**
- Great for immersive nature atmospheres
- Recording in reverberant spaces

## Cardioid / Shotgun
- **Highly directional** - captures what's in front
- Used to "point and shoot" at specific sounds
- Ignores background noise (off-axis rejection)

::right::

## Stereo (XY or ORTF)
- Essential for capturing the **"width"** of a space
- **XY pattern**: Two cardioid mics at 90°
- **ORTF**: Two cardioid mics at 110°, 17cm apart
- Mimics human hearing

### Pattern Comparison

| Pattern | Directionality | Best For |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Omni | 360° | Ambiences |
| Cardioid | Front-focused | Specific sounds |
| Shotgun | Very narrow | Distance |
| Stereo | Wide | Spatial realism |

<!--
Understanding microphone patterns is crucial for choosing the right tool for your recording situation. Each pattern has strengths and weaknesses depending on the environment.
-->

# Wind Protection & Accessories

::left::

## Wind Protection

Outside, even a light breeze can ruin a recording:

- **Foam windscreen** - Basic protection for light wind
- **"Deadcat" / Furry Cover** - Heavy-duty protection
  - Synthetic fur dissipates wind energy
  - Essential for outdoor recording
- **Blimp / Zeppelin** - Complete enclosure
  - Suspended microphone mount
  - Maximum wind protection

::right::

## Essential Accessories

| Accessory | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| **Shock mount** | Reduces handling noise |
| **Pistol grip** | Handheld operation |
| **Boom pole** | Extended reach |
| **Tripod/stand** | Static positioning |
| **Cables** | XLR, 3.5mm adapters |
| **Headphones** | **Monitoring is mandatory** |
| **Storage** | SD cards (Class 10+) |

<!--
Never underestimate the importance of wind protection - it's often the difference between a usable recording and one that's completely destroyed.
-->

# Recording with Mobile Devices
Modern smartphones are surprisingly capable field recording tools.

### Recommended Apps

| App | Platform | Features |
|-----|----------|----------|

### Tips for Mobile Recording

2. **Use external mic**
3. **Monitor with headphones**
4. **Use high-quality settings** - 48kHz/24-bit minimum

# World Building & Diegetic Sound

## World Building
Field recordings provide the **"glue"** that makes a scene feel real:

| **Without Field Recording** | **With Field Recording** |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Sterile, "fake" feeling | Immersive, authentic environment |
| Silent backgrounds | Living, breathing spaces |
| Generic sounds | Specific location character |

**Example:** Forest Scene
- ❌ No background = feels like a studio
- ✅ Birds, wind, leaf rustle = living forest

<!--
Understanding diegetic vs. non-diegetic sound is fundamental to sound design in film and games. Field recordings can serve both purposes.
-->

# Sonic Narrative


## A field recording isn't just "noise"—it tells a story.

::left::

### Same Location, Different Stories

**Frantic City:**
- Lots of honking
- Sirens
- Shouting
- Heavy traffic

**Lonely City:**
- Distant echoes
- Pigeons cooing
- Sparse footsteps
- Subtle wind

::right::

### What Sonic Narrative Reveals:

- **Time of day** - Activity levels, bird songs
- **Season** - Foliage sounds, weather
- **Location type** - Urban vs. rural
- **Emotional tone** - Peaceful, tense, chaotic
- **Human presence** - Crowds, isolation

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-amber-900 rounded text-sm">
<strong>Listen for the story</strong> in every recording. What is the soundscape telling you?
</div>

<!--
Sonic narrative is about intentionality. The same location can tell completely different stories depending on when and how you record it.

# The Field Recording Workflow

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6">

<div>

## 1. Scouting
- Research location's acoustic signature
- Visit at different times
- Note potential interference sources

## 2. Capture
- **Monitoring with headphones is mandatory**
- Check for unwanted interference
  - Distant airplanes
  - Electromagnetic hum
  - Wind noise

</div>

<div>

## 3. Metadata
Label files immediately:

❌ "REC001.wav"

✅ "NYC_Park_Morning_Birds_48k_24bit.wav"

## 4. Processing
- EQ (frequency balancing)
- Compression (dynamic control)
- Pitch-shifting (creative)
- Noise reduction

</div>

</div>

<!--
Following a consistent workflow ensures you capture usable recordings and can find them when you need them.

# Assignment: "Sound Portrait of a Place"

::left::

### Objective
Create a 2-3 minute **sonic narrative** of a location using only original field recordings.

### Requirements
1. **Record at least 3 separate sessions** at your chosen location
2. **Capture minimum 5 distinct sound elements:**
   - 1 Ambience/Atmosphere
   - 2 Discrete Sound Effects
   - 2 Found Sounds or special moments

::right::

3. **Technical Specs:**
   - 48kHz / 24-bit minimum
   - Proper file naming with metadata
   - Document recording settings

4. **Final Deliverable:**
   - Edited soundscape (2-3 minutes)
   - 1-page written reflection

<!--
This assignment gives students hands-on experience with the entire field recording workflow, from capture to final presentation.
